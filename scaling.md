# Scaling from 19% to 80%: Adoption Path

## Redefining the Target

80% means 1,440 of 1,800 clinics actively sharing. Current baseline is 19% (342 clinics). The gap is 1,098 clinics.

## Segmenting the Resistance

Not all non-participants would resist for the same reason. Three segments require different interventions. Below percentages are assumption-based and need further validation.

**Segment A — Free-riders (~40%, 440 clinics).** No strong objection to sharing. The barrier is structural: currently there is no cost to not sharing, so they don't. Symmetric access would change the calculus. Once not sharing means not receiving, this group is likely to convert.

**Segment B — Competition fear (~40%, 440 clinics).** Genuinely believe sharing will cause patient churn. This fear has partial validity and cannot be addressed with promises. It requires data.

**Segment C — Judgment fear (~20%, 218 clinics).** Worry that peers will evaluate their clinical decisions. Once they see what the receiving clinic actually sees (objective measurements only), most of the anxiety resolves.

## Three-Phase Path

**Phase 1 (Months 1–6): Anchor. Target ~35%.**

Primary target: multi-location clinic groups within Segment A. These clinics already have an internal need for cross-site patient records. The network can be positioned as "seamless coordination across your own locations" — sharing with external clinics is a byproduct. This cohort has the lowest adoption friction and the highest data volume, building network content density fast. Founder 2x credit bonus activates in this phase.

**Phase 2 (Months 7–12): Social pressure. Target ~55%.**

Primary target: Segment B clinics. First, therapists at non-participating clinics encounter locked patient histories daily and begin requesting their admin to join — the cost of non-participation becomes visible at the clinical level. Second, six months of Phase 1 data enables Kinetic to show Segment B clinics actual retention metrics from participating clinics, testing the "sharing causes churn" hypothesis with evidence rather than reassurance. The benchmark dashboard reaches statistical reliability in this phase, creating a second independent pull.

**Phase 3 (Months 13–24): Tipping point. Target ~65%.**

Primary target: remaining Segment B and Segment C clinics. Above 60% network coverage, the commercial cost of non-participation exceeds the psychological cost of joining. The platform no longer needs to persuade directly — peer-to-peer referral and therapist-level demand complete the diffusion.

## Honest Assessment of 80%

Reference case: The US CMS Meaningful Use program is the closest analogue. Launched in 2011 with financial incentives, it pushed hospital EHR data-sharing participation from ~9% to ~60% within two years on incentives alone. Penalties introduced in 2015 drove adoption to 96%. Higher participation might require regulatory enforcement.

Kinetic has no regulatory power and no penalty mechanism.

Sensitivity analysis across conversion assumptions:

| Scenario | Seg A | Seg B | Seg C | Final Rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pessimistic | 50% | 30% | 40% | ~47% |
| Base | 65% | 45% | 55% | ~57% |
| Optimistic | 80% | 60% | 70% | ~68% |

Under pure market incentives, 24-month realistic range is 47%–68%. 80% is not a dishonest target, but it needs to be planned in the long term and consider regulatory moving pieces.
